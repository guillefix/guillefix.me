In Information theory, the mutual information between Random variables XXX, and YYY is defined as:

I(X;Y)=∑x,yp(x,y)logp(x,y)p(x)p(y)=Elogp(X,Y)p(X)p(Y)I(X;Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log{\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}} = E \log{\frac{p(X,Y)}{p(X)p(Y)}}I(X;Y)=∑​x,y​​p(x,y)log​p(x)p(y)​​p(x,y)​​=Elog​p(X)p(Y)​​p(X,Y)​​

where EEE denotes expectation. The mutual information measures the amount of information we obtain about XXX by knowing YYY (see result below).

The mutual information between a random variable and itself is equal to its entropy

Some results (video):

I(X;Y)=H(X)−H(X∣Y)=H(Y)−H(Y∣X)I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X)I(X;Y)=H(X)−H(X∣Y)=H(Y)−H(Y∣X)

H(X∣Y)H(X|Y)H(X∣Y) is the Conditional entropy and thus gives you the information about XXX that YYY doesn't give you.
