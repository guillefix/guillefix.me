where x,yx,yx,y denote sites in the cubic lattice and the notation ⟨x,y⟩\langle x,y \rangle⟨x,y⟩ means that the sum is over nearest-neighbor (i.e., adjacent) sites only; mx\mathbf{m}_xm​x​​ denotes the localized magnetic moment (or, more simply, spin), which for now we treat as a classical vector, at site xxx; JxyJ_{xy}J​xy​​ is the magnetic coupling between nearest-neighbor sites xxx and yyy;and h\mathbf{h}h is a uniform external field (which could be zero) acting on the system.

An even simpler formulation is to treat the spins not as vectors, as in (4.2), but as simple binary variables taking on the values ±1\pm 1±1 only. This is known as the EA Ising model:

HJ=−∑⟨x,y⟩Jxyσx⋅σy−h⋅∑xσx\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{J} = -\sum\limits_{\langle x, y \rangle} J_{xy} \sigma_x \cdot \sigma_y - \mathbf{h} \cdot \sum\limits_x \sigma_xH​J​​=−​⟨x,y⟩​∑​​J​xy​​σ​x​​⋅σ​y​​−h⋅​x​∑​​σ​x​​

The difference with the Heisenberg Hamiltonian is that the couplings JxyJ_{xy}J​xy​​ depend on the particular edge ⟨x,y⟩\langle x, y \rangle⟨x,y⟩ and are chosen randomly and independently from a probability distribution, at the initial time, and stay fixed thereafter (Quenched disorder). On the ±J\pm J±J model, there are only two possible values of JxyJ_{xy}J​xy​​. An important question is which properties depend on the specific realization and which don’t?

The EA Hamiltonian is a very simplified model. If you wanted to faithfully model a dilute magnetic alloy, for example, you would use the RKKY coupling between all pairs of spins with the spins situated at random locations (which would then be quenched).
